# Meter & phrasing — line length, stresses, breath

Meter in song is felt as **stress count + breath length**, not scansion homework. Still, knowing the tools keeps lines singable.

---

## 1. Stress-count meter (practical)

Ignore foot names until you need them. Count spoken stresses per line.

```
•  •     •
walk me home in the rain     (3)
•    •     •    •
don't say a word about him   (4)
```

Keep a **home number** per section (e.g. verse 3–4, chorus 2–3) and allow ±1 variance.

---

## 2. Classical feet (recognition only)

| Foot | Pattern | Feel in song |
|---|---|---|
| Iamb | x • | natural English rise (*to•night*) |
| Trochee | • x | punch then fall (*•heart-less*) |
| Anapest | x x • | gallop, pop build (*in the •dark*) |
| Dactyl | • x x | waltz / fall (*•beau-ti-ful*) |
| Spondee | • • | double hit (*•don't •stop*) |

English lyrics mix freely. Rigid single-foot verse often sounds archaic or comic.

---

## 3. Phrasing vs bar lines

A **lyric phrase** ≠ a bar. One lyric phrase may span 2 bars; one bar may hold two short phrases.

**Breath marks:** write where a singer would actually breathe. If a line can't be sung in one breath at the target BPM, split it.

**BPM interaction:**
- 70–90 BPM — longer lines survive
- 110–130 — shorter phrases, more rests
- 140+ — fragments, chants, less syntax

---

## 4. Parallelism and variation

Parallel openings (*I came… / I saw… / I left…*) create groove. Break parallelism on the payoff line.

```
I kept your coffee cup
I kept the hallway light
I kept the fight
I threw the key
```

The break on line 4 is the point.

---

## 5. Caesura (mid-line rest)

A cut inside the line:
- *I loved you | like a rumor*
- *Come back | or don't*

Useful for attitude and for matching a rest in the melody.

---

## 6. Checklist

- [ ] Stress-count consistent enough to groove, varied enough to live?
- [ ] Breath possible at target BPM?
- [ ] Parallel patterns broken at the turn?
- [ ] No line that only works on paper (unspeakable clusters)?
