# Prosody — matching lyric stress to melodic stress

Prosody is the contract between what the line *means* and how it *sings*. When stress lands wrong, the ear hears a lie even if the rhyme is perfect.

Lineage: Pat Pattison (Berklee) — *Writing Better Lyrics*, *Songwriting Without Boundaries*; stable/unstable line endings as structural tools.

---

## 1. What prosody is (working definition)

- **Spoken stress** — which syllables a native speaker punches in ordinary speech.
- **Musical stress** — which beats/notes the melody punches (downbeats, held notes, higher pitch).
- **Good prosody** = spoken stress and musical stress agree on the important words.
- **Bad prosody** = the melody punches a filler (*the*, *and*, *to*) while the meaning-word is buried on a weak beat.

Test: speak the line at conversation speed. Tap the syllables you naturally stress. Those must sit on strong musical positions — or you rewrite the line, not the meaning.

---

## 2. Stable vs unstable line endings

Pattison's core structural lever:

| Ending | Feel | When to use |
|---|---|---|
| **Stable** | lands, resolves, "period" | ends of chorus lines, title hits, section closes |
| **Unstable** | leans forward, "comma" | verse middles, pre-chorus lifts, questions |

**Stable endings** tend to:
- end on a stressed syllable (masculine ending): *night*, *gone*, *home*
- land on tonic or strong chord tone
- finish a grammatical unit

**Unstable endings** tend to:
- end on an unstressed syllable (feminine): *leaving*, *water*, *hollow*
- hang on a non-tonic tone
- leave syntax open (enjambment, incomplete clause)

**Section design rule:** verses can run unstable → unstable → stable; pre-chorus often stays unstable to force the chorus; chorus titles want stable landings.

---

## 3. Stress alignment checklist

1. Mark primary stresses in the spoken line (• = stress):
   - `I •saw you •cross the •street at •dawn`
2. Map the melody's strong beats (1 and 3 in 4/4; or the held notes).
3. Align: each • should hit a strong beat or a long note.
4. If a function word sits on a peak note — move the peak, or rewrite.

**Common failures:**
- Title word on a pickup/weak beat
- Negation (*don't*, *never*) unstressed while the verb is stressed — flips meaning
- Multi-syllable names forced into wrong stress (*Ma•ria* vs *Mar•ia*)

---

## 4. Line weight and number of stresses

Count stresses per line, not syllables (same instinct as RU тоника).

- **2–3 stresses** — spare, conversational, indie/folk default
- **4 stresses** — balanced pop/verse default
- **5+ stresses** — dense; works in rap/patter; risks clutter in sung pop

Vary stress-count between adjacent lines. Four lines of identical stress-count = machine feel.

**Front-heavy vs back-heavy:**
- Front-heavy: stress early, then trail — confessional, falling energy
- Back-heavy: build to a final punch — hook-friendly, punchline shape

---

## 5. Enjambment and mid-line breaks

Enjambment (sense runs past the line break) creates musical asynchrony — useful tension.

```
I left your sweater on the
chair where morning never
reaches
```

The break after *the* / *never* is unstable by design. Don't enjamb randomly; enjamb to hide a rhyme, delay a reveal, or match a melodic suspension.

---

## 6. Prosody repair moves

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| Stress on filler | Swap word order; cut the filler |
| Title won't sit on downbeat | Shorten title; move it earlier in the bar |
| Feminine ending feels weak in chorus | Cut to masculine (*leaving* → *leave*) |
| Line too long for the phrase | Split into two lines; add a rest |
| Melisma mangling a consonant cluster | Open vowels on long notes (*ah/oh/ay*); move clusters to short notes |

---

## 7. Quick ritual before locking a lyric

1. Speak every line cold. Mark stresses.
2. Sing on nonsense (*la*) first — check contour, then drop words in.
3. Circle every stable ending. Do they fall where the section should land?
4. Read the chorus alone. Does the title land like a period?
